01 · Objective

Formula fields

Given a scenario, demonstrate the use of formula fields to meet stated business requirements.

A formula field is a read-only value calculated at runtime — never stored, always current. It's the first tool to reach for whenever a scenario says "display" or "calculate" a value from data already on (or above) the record.

Core characteristics

  • Read-only, recalculated whenever the record is viewed or queried — so it's never stale, and never editable by users or automation.
  • Return types: Text, Number, Currency, Percent, Date, Date/Time, Time, Checkbox (boolean).
  • Can reference the record's own fields and — via cross-object formulas — fields on parent records up the relationship chain.
◆ Cross-object formulas go UP, not down

Formulas can traverse up to 10 relationships to pull a parent's field (show the Account's Industry on a Contact). They go up from child to parent only — never down to children. To aggregate children you need a roll-up summary, not a formula.

Functions worth recognising

  • IF, CASE — branching logic.
  • ISBLANK / ISNULL — emptiness checks.
  • TEXT, VALUE, DATE, TODAY, NOW — conversions and dates.
  • ISPICKVAL, INCLUDES — picklist and multi-select checks.
  • HYPERLINK, IMAGE — rich display in the field.

Note: ISCHANGED, PRIORVALUE, ISNEW are not available in formula fields — they belong to validation rules and flows, because a formula field has no notion of "before".

▲ Exam trap

A formula field cannot reference or aggregate child records (that's a roll-up), cannot be edited or set by automation, and stores no history. The compiled formula size limit (~3,900 characters / 5,000 bytes) is real for very complex formulas.

Worked scenario

On the Contact, sales want a read-only "Account Tier" that always reflects the parent Account's Tier field, and a colour-coded status. Build?

Reasoning → Answer

A cross-object formula field on Contact referencing Account.Tier__c, using CASE (or IMAGE) for the colour coding. No automation needed — it stays current automatically, and can't drift out of sync the way a copied field would.

◆ Chapter recap
  • Formula fields are read-only, runtime-calculated, never stored, always current.
  • Cross-object formulas reference parent fields up to 10 relationships up — never children.
  • No ISCHANGED/PRIORVALUE/ISNEW in formulas; no child aggregation; no history.
  • Prefer a formula over automation-copied fields to avoid drift.
Self-check.
Q1Can a formula field aggregate a sum of child records?
No — that's a roll-up summary. — Formulas only traverse up to parents.
Q2Can automation write to a formula field?
No — formula fields are read-only. — They recalculate at runtime.
Q3You need to show a parent Account field on the Contact, always current. Tool?
A cross-object formula field. — It reads the parent value live, avoiding sync drift.
02 · Objective

Roll-up summary fields

Given a scenario, determine the capabilities, use cases, and implications of roll-up summary fields.

A roll-up summary is the only declarative way to aggregate child records onto a parent — but it comes with one hard requirement that dominates the exam questions.

What they do

A roll-up summary sits on the parent and aggregates values from its child records using COUNT, SUM, MIN, or MAX. The value is stored and recalculated automatically as children change.

◆ The hard requirement

Roll-up summaries require a master-detail relationship (child → parent). You cannot create one over a plain lookup. A few standard exceptions exist (e.g. Opportunity → Account), but for custom modelling the rule is simple: no master-detail, no roll-up.

Capabilities and limits

  • You can filter which child records are included (criteria).
  • COUNT works on any child; SUM/MIN/MAX need a numeric, currency, or date field.
  • Certain fields can't be rolled up — e.g. long text areas, and some cross-object formula fields on the child.
  • Roll-up recalculation can re-trigger the parent's own automation — a recursion risk (see the errors chapter).
▲ Exam trap — the lookup workaround

"Sum child amounts onto the parent, but the relationship is a lookup" → you must either (a) change it to master-detail, or (b) maintain the total with a record-triggered Flow (or AppExchange tool / Apex). Flow is the declarative workaround when master-detail isn't possible.

Worked scenario

An Account should show the count of related "Support Tickets" and the sum of their hours — but Tickets are linked by a lookup because a Ticket can be reassigned to a different Account. What are the options?

Reasoning → Answer

A roll-up summary is impossible over a lookup. Options: (1) convert the relationship to master-detail (but that loses easy reparenting and independent ownership), or (2) keep the lookup and use a record-triggered Flow to maintain Count and Sum on the Account. Given the reassignment requirement, the Flow approach preserves the lookup's flexibility.

◆ Chapter recap
  • Roll-up summaries aggregate children onto the parent: COUNT, SUM, MIN, MAX.
  • They require a master-detail relationship (a few standard exceptions aside).
  • Over a lookup, use a record-triggered Flow (or Apex) to maintain the total instead.
  • Roll-up recalculation can re-trigger parent automation — watch for recursion.
Self-check.
Q1Which four functions can a roll-up summary use?
COUNT, SUM, MIN, MAX. — On a master-detail child.
Q2The relationship is a lookup but you need a running total on the parent. Declarative option?
A record-triggered Flow to maintain the total. — Roll-ups need master-detail; Flow is the declarative fallback.
03 · Objective

Validation rules

Given a scenario, demonstrate the use of validation rules to meet stated business requirements.

Validation rules are the platform's gatekeeper for data quality. A rule is a formula that, when it evaluates to TRUE, blocks the save and shows an error.

How they work

  • The formula returns TRUE/FALSE. TRUE = error — the save is stopped and the message displayed.
  • They fire on insert and update, at the point of save.
  • Errors can appear at the top of the page or next to a specific field.
◆ Write the invalid condition

Describe the state you want to block. "Close Date must be in the future" becomes a rule that fires when CloseDate < TODAY(). The formula is the error condition, not the valid one.

Functions that make validation powerful

  • ISCHANGED(field) — enforce only when a field changes.
  • PRIORVALUE(field) — compare to the pre-edit value (e.g. block moving a Stage backwards).
  • ISNEW() — enforce only on creation.
  • ISPICKVAL, ISBLANK, REGEX — value checks and format enforcement.
  • $Profile / $Permission / $User — exempt certain users (e.g. an integration account).
▲ Exam trap — validation fires on automation too

Validation rules also run on records created/updated by imports, the API, and automation. A badly scoped rule can break a data load or a Flow. To exempt an integration user, gate the rule with $User / $Profile. Also: cross-object validation can reference parent fields (up the master-detail), not child records.

Worked scenario

Reps must not move an Opportunity from "Closed Won" back to an open stage, but the nightly integration user must be allowed to update these records freely. Build?

Reasoning → Answer

A validation rule using ISCHANGED(StageName) and PRIORVALUE(StageName) = "Closed Won" to block the backward move — combined with $Profile.Name <> "Integration" so the integration user is exempt. TRUE (for non-integration users making the illegal change) blocks the save.

◆ Chapter recap
  • Validation rule formula TRUE = blocked save with an error message.
  • ISCHANGED, PRIORVALUE, ISNEW enable state-aware and creation-only rules.
  • Rules fire on imports, API, and automation too — exempt integrations via $User/$Profile.
  • Cross-object validation reaches parent fields, not children.
Self-check.
Q1A validation rule returns TRUE for a record. What happens?
The save is blocked and the error shown. — TRUE is the error condition.
Q2How do you stop a rule from breaking an integration user's data loads?
Gate it with $User or $Profile to exempt that user. — Rules otherwise fire on API/automation.
Q3Which function compares a field to its value before the edit?
PRIORVALUE. — Useful for blocking backward stage moves.
04 · Objective

Approval processes

Given a scenario, determine the capabilities and use cases for approval processes.

An approval process is a structured, multi-step sign-off engine. It's the right — and only — declarative answer whenever a record must be formally approved before it can progress.

Anatomy

  • Entry criteria — which records may enter (Discount > 20%).
  • Approval steps — one or more, each with an approver: a specific user, a role, the submitter's manager (via a hierarchy field), a queue, or a dynamically chosen related user.
  • Actions at each transition — Initial Submission, Approval, Rejection, Recall — each can fire field updates, email alerts, tasks, or outbound messages.
  • Approver experience — approve/reject by email, in-app, or in the Salesforce mobile app; delegated approvers supported.
◆ Signature capabilities no other tool has

Approval processes uniquely provide record locking during approval, multi-step routing, parallel approvers (all or any must approve), dynamic approver assignment, and an approval history related list. No Flow or validation rule gives you formal, auditable sign-off.

▲ Exam trap

When a scenario needs a human to formally approve before something proceeds (a discount, an expense, a contract) → approval process, not a Flow. A Flow can submit a record into an approval and can run on final approval, but the sign-off engine itself is the approval process. Records can be locked automatically while pending.

Worked scenario

Any Opportunity with a discount over 20% must be approved by the deal desk, then by the regional VP, and must be locked from edits while under review. What do you build, and how might a Flow assist?

Reasoning → Answer

A two-step approval process (deal desk → VP) with entry criteria on discount and record locking enabled. A record-triggered Flow can auto-submit the Opportunity when the discount crosses 20%, but the approval routing, locking, and history live in the approval process.

◆ Chapter recap
  • Approval processes are the only declarative tool for formal, auditable human sign-off.
  • They provide record locking, multi-step and parallel routing, dynamic approvers, and history.
  • Actions fire on submission, approval, rejection, and recall.
  • Flow can auto-submit into an approval and act on the outcome, but isn't the sign-off engine.
Self-check.
Q1A discount must be formally approved by two managers before the deal proceeds. Tool?
An approval process. — It provides multi-step sign-off, locking, and history.
Q2How can automation start an approval when criteria are met?
A record-triggered Flow can auto-submit the record for approval. — The approval process still owns the routing and locking.
05 · Objective

Determining the right automation tool

Given a scenario, determine the tool or action needed to meet business requirements.

The exam relentlessly asks "which automation tool?". In the current platform the default answer is Flow — and knowing the before-save vs after-save split resolves most of the harder questions.

◆ The Flow-first reality

Salesforce has retired the creation of new Workflow Rules and Process Builder processes. Flow is the tool for anything new. Legacy Workflow/Process Builder may still appear in scenarios (for updating existing automations), but you never build new ones.

The declarative toolset, by job

RequirementTool
Block a save when data is invalidValidation rule
Calculate / display a value (no storage)Formula field
Aggregate children onto a master-detail parentRoll-up summary
Formal human sign-offApproval process
Update fields / create records / send email on a triggerRecord-triggered Flow
Guided, multi-screen data entry / wizardScreen Flow
Run on a schedule / batch of recordsSchedule-triggered Flow
React to a platform eventPlatform Event-triggered Flow
Reusable logic called from other flows/ApexAutolaunched Flow
Callouts / complex bulk / rollback / logic Flow can't expressApex

Record-triggered Flow: before-save vs after-save

Before-save (fast field update)After-save
Use forUpdating fields on the same record that triggered itRelated records, emails, sending to approval, async actions
PerformanceMuch faster — no extra DMLHeavier (a separate save)
Can't doActions, sub-flows, related-record updates, emails—
▲ Exam trap

"Update a field on the same record as efficiently as possible" → before-save record-triggered Flow (the modern, faster equivalent of a Workflow field update). "Update a related record or send an email" → after-save. Choosing after-save for a same-record update is a classic wrong answer.

Worked scenario

When a Case is created, stamp a "Response Due" date on the same Case (24h out) and also notify the account owner by email. One Flow or two behaviours?

Reasoning → Answer

Two behaviours across the split: the same-record field stamp belongs in a before-save flow (fast, no extra DML); the email to the account owner needs an after-save flow (emails and related-record work aren't allowed before save). In practice, a before-save flow for the date and an after-save flow for the notification.

◆ Chapter recap
  • New automation = Flow; Workflow Rules and Process Builder can no longer be created.
  • Match the tool to the job: validation, formula, roll-up, approval, or a Flow variant.
  • Before-save Flow = fastest way to set fields on the same record; after-save for related records/email/approval.
  • Drop to Apex only for callouts, complex bulk, or logic Flow can't express.
Self-check.
Q1Fastest way to default a field on the same record on save?
A before-save record-triggered Flow. — No extra DML; runs earliest.
Q2Can you create a new Workflow Rule today?
No. — Salesforce retired new Workflow/Process Builder creation; use Flow.
Q3You need to send an email and update a related record when a record changes. Before-save or after-save Flow?
After-save. — Before-save can't send emails or touch related records.
06 · Objective

Automating without errors

Given a set of business requirements, recommend a solution to automate business processes while avoiding errors in automation.

When several automations touch the same save, order and recursion decide whether the system behaves or spirals. This chapter is where the harder automation questions are won.

Order of execution — the map

On every save, Salesforce runs steps in a fixed order. A simplified, exam-relevant sequence:

  1. Loads the record and overwrites with new values; runs system validation (required fields, formats).
  2. Before-save record-triggered Flows.
  3. Before Apex triggers.
  4. System + custom validation rules; duplicate rules.
  5. Record saved (not yet committed).
  6. After Apex triggers.
  7. Assignment rules, auto-response rules.
  8. Workflow rules (legacy) — a workflow field update re-fires before/after triggers once.
  9. After-save record-triggered Flows and processes.
  10. Escalation rules; roll-up summary recalculation on the parent (which can trigger the parent's own automation).
  11. Criteria-based sharing evaluation.
  12. Commit to the database; then post-commit work (emails, async/@future).
◆ Why it matters

Two automations writing the same fields can overwrite each other or fire in an order you didn't expect. The sequence explains puzzles like "why did my validation rule reject a value my flow was supposed to set?" — because the flow ran after validation, or vice-versa.

Recursion — the classic failure

▲ The recursion loop

Automation A updates Field 1 → that triggers Automation B, which updates Field 2 → which re-triggers A… → an infinite loop that hits governor limits and errors. It's especially easy when field updates, roll-ups, and record-triggered flows all touch related records.

How to avoid it

  • Gate with entry criteria / ISCHANGED so automation only runs when the relevant field actually changed.
  • Prefer one before-save flow per object for same-record field updates rather than several overlapping automations.
  • Avoid multiple tools writing the same fields; consolidate the logic.
  • Watch roll-up recalculation re-triggering parent automation.
  • Test in a sandbox at realistic data volumes to surface governor-limit failures early.
◆ Rule of thumb

One object, one before-save flow for field defaults; keep logic consolidated and entry criteria tight. Fewer tools touching a save means fewer surprises.

Worked scenario

An admin has three record-triggered flows on Account, each updating a rating field, plus a roll-up that recalculates on child changes. Users report intermittent "too many SOQL/DML" errors on save. What's likely wrong and how do you fix it?

Reasoning → Answer

Overlapping automations are almost certainly re-triggering each other (recursion) and stacking operations past governor limits. Consolidate the rating logic into a single before-save flow, gate each branch with ISCHANGED / entry criteria, and check whether the roll-up recalculation is re-firing the flows. Then load-test in a sandbox.

◆ Chapter recap
  • Know the order of execution: before-save flow → before trigger → validation/dup → save → after trigger → workflow → after-save flow → roll-up → commit → emails.
  • Recursion: automations re-triggering each other until governor limits error the save.
  • Prevent it: tight entry criteria/ISCHANGED, one before-save flow per object, no duplicate field writers.
  • Watch roll-up recalculation re-firing parent automation; test at volume.
Self-check.
Q1What runs first on save — a before-save flow or a validation rule?
The before-save flow. — It runs before custom validation, so it can set a value the rule then checks.
Q2What causes an automation recursion loop?
Automations that update fields which re-trigger each other in a cycle. — Gate with ISCHANGED/entry criteria and consolidate logic.
Q3Best practice for same-record field defaults to minimise conflicts?
One before-save flow per object with tight entry criteria. — Fewer overlapping writers means fewer surprises and no extra DML.
