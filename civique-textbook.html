Chapter 1: Principles and Values of the Republic

1.1 The Republic and Its National Symbols

France has been a republic, with a few interruptions, since 1792, and today's institutions trace back to the Fifth Republic, established in 1958. A republic is a form of government in which the head of state is not a hereditary monarch but is chosen, directly or indirectly, by the citizens, and in which sovereignty belongs to the people rather than to a single ruling family. This distinguishes France sharply from its own history before the Revolution, when the country was ruled by kings who claimed to govern by divine right.

Every republic tends to develop a set of symbols that citizens can recognize and rally around, and France is particularly rich in these. The most visible is the tricolor flag, with its three vertical bands of blue, white, and red. The colors are generally explained as a combination of the traditional colors of the city of Paris (blue and red) with the white of the monarchy, symbolically merged during the Revolution to represent national unity between the people and the crown-turned-nation. The flag flies over every town hall, school, and government building in the country and is a mandatory part of official ceremonies.

The national anthem, La Marseillaise, was composed in 1792 by Claude Joseph Rouget de Lisle as a marching song for French revolutionary troops and takes its name from the volunteers of Marseille who sang it as they marched into Paris. It became the national anthem in 1795, was suppressed under the monarchy and empire at various points, and was permanently reinstated as the anthem of France in 1879. Its lyrics are martial and revolutionary in tone, calling citizens to arms in defense of the homeland.

Marianne is the female figure who personifies the French Republic itself, much as Britannia represents the United Kingdom or Uncle Sam represents the United States. She is typically depicted wearing a Phrygian cap, a soft conical cap that was worn by freed slaves in antiquity and was adopted during the Revolution as a symbol of liberty. Busts of Marianne are displayed in every town hall in France, and her image has appeared on coins, stamps, and official seals; since the 1970s, the face used for Marianne has often been modeled on a well-known living Frenchwoman.

The rooster (le coq gaulois) is another widely recognized, though less official, symbol of France, rooted in a Latin pun: the word "gallus" meant both "rooster" and "inhabitant of Gaul." It is especially associated with French sporting teams. Finally, the national motto, "Liberté, Égalité, Fraternité" (Liberty, Equality, Fraternity), dates to the Revolutionary period and was written into the Constitution of 1958 as the Republic's official motto; it commonly appears carved into the pediments of public buildings alongside the Marianne bust and the flag.

1.2 Liberty, Equality, Fraternity: Unpacking the Motto

Each word of the motto carries specific legal and civic weight in French life, and exam questions frequently probe the precise meaning of each term rather than the phrase as a whole.

Liberty refers to the right of every person to act, think, believe, and express themselves as they choose, provided they do not harm others or break the law. In practice this covers freedoms such as freedom of expression, freedom of movement, freedom of association, and freedom of conscience. French law recognizes that liberty is not absolute: it can be restricted when its exercise would infringe on the rights of others or threaten public order. This is why, for instance, freedom of expression does not protect speech that incites hatred or violence, or that constitutes defamation.

Equality means that all citizens possess exactly the same rights and are subject to exactly the same duties, regardless of characteristics such as sex, origin, disability, religion, or physical appearance. This principle underlies anti-discrimination law in France: it is illegal to publicly insult a person because they are different, and it is illegal to reserve certain jobs or professions exclusively for men (or exclusively for women). Equality before the law is considered one of the bedrock achievements of the 1789 Revolution, which abolished the legal privileges previously enjoyed by the nobility and clergy.

Fraternity is the least legally precise of the three terms but is generally understood as solidarity: a sense of mutual responsibility and support that binds citizens together as members of the same national community. This value is expressed concretely through France's social welfare system, its public health insurance, and the general expectation that citizens will look out for one another, for example by assisting someone in danger or contributing to organizations (associations) that serve community needs.

1.3 Laïcité: The Separation of Religion and State

Laïcité, often translated as "secularism," is one of the most distinctive and frequently tested elements of French civic identity. It is enshrined in Article 1 of the 1958 Constitution, which states that France is "an indivisible, secular (laïque), democratic and social Republic." The principle has two complementary sides. First, the state itself is religiously neutral: it does not recognize, fund, or favor any religion, and public institutions and their employees must remain neutral in religious matters while performing their duties. Second, laïcité guarantees freedom of conscience to every individual: everyone is free to hold any religious belief, to hold none at all, or to change their religion, and the state protects this freedom rather than dictating belief.

The legal foundation of modern laïcité is the law of December 9, 1905, on the separation of the Churches and the State, which ended the official status and funding that the state had previously extended to the Catholic Church (and, in different ways, to other recognized faiths) and established that the Republic "does not recognize, pay, or subsidize any religion." This law was a major turning point after the intense political conflicts of the 19th century between clerical and anti-clerical factions.

Laïcité is applied with particular strictness in public schools, because schools are seen as a space where children of every background must be treated identically and must not feel pressured, one way or another, regarding religion. This is why school attendance is compulsory for all children regardless of their family's religion, and a child cannot lawfully refuse to attend school for a religious reason. It is also why displays of religious affiliation by staff, and certain conspicuous religious symbols by pupils, are restricted in public schools, a topic that has generated significant public debate in France over the past few decades. At the same time, laïcité is not hostility to religion: individuals retain full freedom to practice their faith (or no faith) in their private lives and in society at large.

1.4 Freedom of Expression and Its Limits

Freedom of expression is one of the freedoms most closely tied to the Revolutionary heritage, formally guaranteed by Article 11 of the 1789 Declaration of the Rights of Man and Citizen. It allows individuals to hold and share opinions, including opinions that are critical of the government, religion, or public figures. However, French law draws clear lines: expression that constitutes incitement to hatred or violence against a person or group, defamation, or public insult on the basis of protected characteristics is not protected speech and can be subject to civil or criminal penalties. Exam questions on this topic typically test whether candidates understand that freedom of expression is real but not unlimited.

Chapter 2: Institutional and Political System

2.1 The Fifth Republic: A Semi-Presidential System

France is governed under the Constitution of the Fifth Republic, adopted in 1958 largely at the initiative of General Charles de Gaulle, who became its founder and first elected President. The Fifth Republic replaced the Fourth Republic (1946-1958), whose parliamentary system had been criticized as unstable, prone to frequent changes of government, and ill-equipped to manage crises such as the war in Algeria. De Gaulle's new Constitution created a stronger executive, centered on a President of the Republic with real governing powers, while retaining a Parliament and a Prime Minister responsible to it — a hybrid arrangement often described as "semi-presidential."

France has had five republics since 1792 (interspersed with two empires and periods of monarchy), and today's system is often summarized as combining a powerful, directly elected President with a Prime Minister and government that must retain the confidence of the National Assembly. This dual structure is a frequent source of exam questions, since it can initially seem to blur the line between who is "in charge."

2.2 The Executive Branch

Executive power in France is shared between the President of the Republic and the Government, led by the Prime Minister. The President is elected directly by French citizens for a five-year term (this term length, known as the "quinquennat," replaced the previous seven-year term in a 2000 constitutional reform). The President resides and works at the Élysée Palace in Paris, appoints the Prime Minister, presides over the Council of Ministers, is the commander-in-chief of the armed forces, and represents France abroad.

The Prime Minister, appointed by the President, leads the Government and is formally responsible for its day-to-day policy and administration, though in practice the President — especially when their own political party controls the National Assembly — sets much of the overall direction. The Government as a whole (Prime Minister plus ministers) is accountable to the National Assembly, which can force it to resign through a vote of no confidence. If a minister breaks the law in the course of their duties, they are not above the law: like any citizen, they can be investigated and prosecuted (ministers face a special court, the Cour de Justice de la République, for offenses committed in office).

2.3 The Legislative Branch: Parliament

The French Parliament is bicameral, meaning it is composed of two chambers: the National Assembly (Assemblée nationale) and the Senate (Sénat). The National Assembly has 577 members, called deputies (députés), each elected directly by voters in a single-member constituency for a five-year term, unless the President dissolves the Assembly earlier and calls new elections. The National Assembly is generally considered the more powerful chamber, since it can bring down a government through a no-confidence vote and has the final say in the event of disagreement with the Senate over legislation.

The Senate is composed of senators elected not directly by the public but by an electoral college of local elected officials (mayors, regional and departmental councillors, and delegates), in a system of indirect universal suffrage. Senators serve six-year terms, with roughly half the Senate renewed every three years. The Senate represents France's territorial communities (regions, departments, and towns) and reviews and amends legislation, though the National Assembly can ultimately override it on most bills.

Together, Parliament's principal roles are to debate and vote on laws and to oversee (control) the actions of the Government. Deputies and senators can question ministers, form investigative committees, and vote on the national budget.

2.4 The Judicial Branch

The judicial authority (autorité judiciaire) in France is exercised by judges and the court system, and its role is to apply the law, resolve disputes between individuals, and administer criminal justice, including sanctioning those who commit offenses such as theft. Judicial power is considered a distinct branch from the executive and legislative branches, reflecting the classic principle of the separation of powers, which holds that concentrating too much authority in a single body or person threatens liberty. No one, including the President, holds unlimited power in the French system; each of the three powers acts as a check on excesses by the others.

2.5 Elections and Voting

To vote in French elections, a person must generally be a French citizen, at least 18 years old, and registered on the electoral rolls; they must also not have been stripped of their civic rights by a court. This is the foundation of what the French call "suffrage universel" (universal suffrage): the principle that the right to vote belongs to all adult citizens equally, without restrictions based on wealth, education, or social status — a principle that was itself the product of long historical struggle, since voting in France was for a time limited to men who paid a minimum amount of tax (the property-based "suffrage censitaire").

Different elections serve different purposes. Presidential elections choose the President of the Republic. Legislative elections choose the 577 deputies of the National Assembly. Municipal elections choose municipal councillors, who in turn select the mayor (maire) to lead the commune (town or city). France is divided into roughly 101 departments (including five overseas departments), each administered locally by an elected departmental council and represented, on behalf of the national government, by a préfet — a senior civil servant appointed by the Council of Ministers who ensures that national law is respected within the department and coordinates state services there. Political parties are free to form and operate in France, reflecting the country's commitment to political pluralism.

2.6 France and the European Union

France was one of the founding members of European integration, alongside Germany, Italy, Belgium, the Netherlands, and Luxembourg, when the European Coal and Steel Community and later the European Economic Community (EEC, created by the Treaty of Rome in 1957) were established. Today France belongs to the European Union (EU), which as of January 1, 2025, comprises 27 member states. EU citizens (including French citizens) directly elect members of the European Parliament every five years, and the euro is the official currency used in France, replacing the French franc in the early 2000s. Europe Day, marking the anniversary of the 1950 Schuman Declaration that set European integration in motion, is celebrated on May 9.

Chapter 3: Rights and Duties

3.1 The Foundational Texts of French Rights

The bedrock legal text for individual rights in France is the Declaration of the Rights of Man and Citizen (Déclaration des droits de l'homme et du citoyen), adopted by the National Constituent Assembly on August 26, 1789, in the early, most idealistic phase of the French Revolution. Directly inspired by Enlightenment philosophy and by the American Declaration of Independence and Bill of Rights, the 1789 Declaration proclaims that "men are born and remain free and equal in rights," and it enumerates rights including liberty, property, security, and resistance to oppression. It remains legally binding today: it is cited in the preamble of the 1958 Constitution and is treated by French courts as part of the "bloc de constitutionnalité," the body of texts against which the constitutionality of laws is measured.

France's current Constitution — the founding text of the Fifth Republic — was adopted in 1958. It establishes the organization of the state (as covered in Chapter 2) and, through its preamble, incorporates both the 1789 Declaration and the social and economic rights set out in the preamble to the 1946 Constitution (such as the right to strike, the right to join a union, and the right to social protection). Together, these texts form the constitutional foundation for the rights and duties of everyone residing in France.

3.2 Individual Freedoms and Their Limits

French law recognizes a broad set of individual freedoms: freedom of expression, freedom of conscience and religion, freedom of movement, freedom of association, and the right to privacy, among others. Freedom of expression, in particular, is the right to express one's opinions and ideas freely, but — as discussed in Chapter 1 — it is not absolute. More broadly, French legal tradition holds that individual freedoms may be limited by law, but only to the extent necessary to protect public order, the rights and freedoms of other people, or public safety and health. This balancing test appears repeatedly across different rights: for example, freedom of movement can be restricted during a declared public health emergency, and property rights can be limited by planning and environmental regulation.

Several specific areas of personal and family law are commonly tested. Abortion (interruption volontaire de grossesse, IVG) is legal in France; access to it was first guaranteed by the 1975 Veil Law and has since been reinforced, including its 2024 inscription into the French Constitution as a guaranteed freedom, making France the first country to grant abortion explicit constitutional protection. Divorce is legally available in France through several distinct procedures (including mutual consent, an accepted breakdown of the marriage, and fault-based divorce), so a marriage can always end in divorce even if one spouse objects, subject to legal process. Polygamy — being legally married to more than one person at the same time — is illegal in France. The death penalty was definitively abolished in France in 1981, under President François Mitterrand and Justice Minister Robert Badinter, making France one of the later Western European countries to do so, but firmly aligning it with the rest of the EU, where capital punishment is now prohibited.

3.3 Civic Duties and the Rule of Law

Rights in the French system are paired with duties. Every person present in France — citizen or not — is required to respect the law; there is no right to simply disregard a law one disagrees with, though citizens can challenge laws through legal and democratic channels (courts, elections, petitions, protest within legal limits). A person who fails to respect the law faces criminal penalties proportional to the seriousness of the offense, which can range from a fine to imprisonment.

French criminal law divides offenses (infractions) into three tiers of increasing seriousness: contraventions (minor offenses, such as small traffic violations, generally punished only by fines and handled by police courts), délits (mid-level offenses such as theft or assault, judged by the tribunal correctionnel and punishable by fines and/or prison sentences up to ten years), and crimes (the most serious offenses, such as murder or armed robbery, tried before a jury at the Cour d'assises and carrying the heaviest sentences, including life imprisonment). Understanding this three-tier hierarchy helps explain many exam questions about "which offense is more serious" or "who judges a given case."

Two law-enforcement bodies share responsibility for public order in France: the police nationale, a civilian force generally operating in urban areas, and the gendarmerie nationale, a force with military status that traditionally covers rural areas and smaller towns, along with certain specialized national missions. Both work under judicial supervision when investigating crimes.

Civic duty also extends to everyday obligations: a person who witnesses or is involved in an accident must help the victim and alert emergency services; failing to assist a person in danger is itself a criminal offense in France (non-assistance à personne en danger). Citizens are also expected to act responsibly toward the environment and community — for example, by reducing and properly sorting waste, and refraining from littering (throwing a bottle in the street) or illegal dumping (leaving bulky items such as a broken washing machine on the sidewalk), both of which are punishable offenses.

3.4 Protecting Victims and Vulnerable People

French law places special emphasis on protecting victims of violence and exploitation. A victim of violence should report the offense to the police or gendarmerie (filing what is called a "plainte") and can access medical care and victim-support associations that provide legal, psychological, and practical assistance, often free of charge. Human trafficking (la traite des êtres humains) — the exploitation of a person through force, deception, or coercion, for purposes such as forced labor, forced begging, or sexual exploitation — is a serious crime under French and international law, and France has dedicated services to identify and support victims of trafficking.

Finally, French citizenship itself confers a specific bundle of rights not extended to all residents: the right to vote and stand for office, the right to hold a French passport and receive consular protection abroad, and full protection under French nationality law. Foreign residents who are not citizens still enjoy the broad protections of French and European human rights law, including access to justice, but certain political rights (such as voting in national elections) remain reserved for citizens.

Chapter 4: History, Geography and Culture

4.1 From Monarchy to Revolution

For centuries before 1789, France was ruled by an absolute monarchy, in which the king held sweeping powers, often justified by the idea of divine right. By the late 1780s, the monarchy under King Louis XVI faced a severe financial crisis, an unfair and unequal tax system that spared the nobility and clergy while burdening ordinary people, and rising influence from Enlightenment ideas about liberty, equality, and popular sovereignty. These pressures came to a head in 1789: the calling of the Estates-General, the formation of the National Assembly, and the storming of the Bastille fortress-prison on July 14, 1789 — an event that has come to symbolize the uprising against royal authority and is commemorated every year as France's national holiday. The Revolution abolished feudal privileges, produced the Declaration of the Rights of Man and Citizen, and, within a few years, ended the monarchy outright: Louis XVI was tried and executed in 1793, after the First Republic had already been proclaimed in September 1792.

The revolutionary period was turbulent, including the radical Terror under Robespierre, before power eventually consolidated around a young general, Napoleon Bonaparte, who seized power in a coup in 1799 and crowned himself Emperor of the French in 1804, becoming Napoleon I. Napoleon is remembered both for exporting revolutionary legal reforms across Europe (notably the Napoleonic Code, which reorganized civil law and influenced legal systems worldwide) and for years of near-continuous warfare that reshaped the map of Europe before his final defeat at Waterloo in 1815.

4.2 From Empire to the Five Republics

After Napoleon's fall, France cycled through several different regimes over the following century and a half: a restored monarchy, a briefer Second Republic (1848), a Second Empire under Napoleon III, and eventually the Third Republic, established in 1870, which endured through the First World War and the interwar period until France's defeat by Nazi Germany in 1940. France has, in total, had five republics. The Fourth Republic followed the Second World War (1946), and the Fifth Republic — the current system — was established in 1958 under Charles de Gaulle, who became its founder and first elected President in direct universal suffrage (from 1965 onward).

Along the way, several landmark reforms defined French society as it exists today. Slavery, which had been briefly abolished during the Revolution in 1794 and then reinstated by Napoleon in 1802, was definitively and permanently abolished in France and its colonies in 1848, following a campaign led by figures such as Victor Schœlcher. Free, secular, and compulsory public education was established through a series of laws in the 1880s associated with the Minister of Public Instruction Jules Ferry; public primary education became free in 1881 and compulsory (for children up to a certain age) in 1882, laying the foundation for the modern, universal, secular school system described further in Chapter 5.

4.3 The World Wars and Their Legacy

The First World War (1914-1918) was fought largely on French soil along the Western Front and resulted in enormous French casualties; the armistice that ended the fighting was signed on November 11, 1918, a date still commemorated every year in France as a public holiday honoring the war dead. The Second World War (1939-1945) brought the German occupation of France from 1940 to 1944, the collaborationist Vichy regime, and the parallel Free French resistance movement led from exile by Charles de Gaulle. This period also saw the Shoah — the systematic genocide of six million European Jews carried out by Nazi Germany and its collaborators, including the deportation of tens of thousands of Jews from France itself — a historical tragedy that France, like the rest of Europe, formally commemorates and studies as a matter of civic education.

In the aftermath of the war, France, having itself been at the heart of two catastrophic European conflicts within a generation, became one of the driving forces behind European integration, intended to make future wars between European powers unthinkable by binding their economies together. This culminated in the creation of the European Economic Community by the 1957 Treaty of Rome, a direct ancestor of today's European Union (see Chapter 2). France also began a gradual process of decolonization during this period: it had built a large colonial empire over previous centuries, including territories in North Africa (notably Algeria, which fought a long and traumatic war of independence from 1954 to 1962), West and Central Africa, Southeast Asia (Indochina, comprising modern Vietnam, Laos, and Cambodia), and the Caribbean, most of which gained independence between the late 1940s and the 1960s.

4.4 Geography of France

Metropolitan France (the part of France located in Europe, as distinct from its overseas territories) sits on the western edge of the European continent. It borders the Atlantic Ocean to the west, the English Channel to the north, and the Mediterranean Sea to the south. Its southern border with Spain runs along the Pyrenees mountain range, while its border with Italy runs through the Alps, home to Mont Blanc, the highest peak in Western Europe. Major rivers include the Seine (which flows through Paris), the Loire, the Rhône, and the Garonne. Metropolitan France is divided into 13 administrative regions and, as noted in Chapter 2, roughly 101 departments in total, since several departments are located overseas.

Paris, situated on the Seine in the north of the country, is the capital of France and its largest city, serving as the seat of government, home to the President's official residence (the Élysée Palace, per Chapter 2) and to landmark cultural sites such as the Eiffel Tower and the Louvre — the world's most-visited art museum, housed in a former royal palace and famous for holdings such as the Mona Lisa. Other major French cities include Marseille, a large port city on the Mediterranean coast and France's second-largest city; Lyon, a historic city at the confluence of the Rhône and Saône rivers; and Nice, another prominent Mediterranean coastal city. Corsica, the birthplace of Napoleon, is a mountainous French island located in the Mediterranean Sea. Beyond metropolitan France, French overseas departments and territories include islands and regions such as Guadeloupe, Martinique, French Guiana, Mayotte, and Réunion, whose residents are full French citizens.

4.5 French Cultural Figures

The exam's cultural questions draw on a broad roster of writers, artists, and performers who shaped French and world culture. In literature, Molière (Jean-Baptiste Poquelin) was the great comic playwright and actor of the 17th century, still widely performed today; Jean de la Fontaine, a contemporary, is famous for his verse Fables, which use animal characters to convey moral lessons; Victor Hugo, in the 19th century, wrote monumental novels such as "Les Misérables" and "The Hunchback of Notre-Dame" and was also a leading poet and political figure; Charles Baudelaire, another 19th-century poet, is best known for the landmark collection "Les Fleurs du mal"; George Sand was the pen name of Amantine Lucile Dupin, a prolific 19th-century novelist notable for defying the gender conventions of her era; Albert Camus, a 20th-century French-Algerian novelist and philosopher associated with existentialism and absurdism, wrote "The Stranger" and won the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1957; and Simone de Beauvoir, a 20th-century philosopher and pioneering feminist thinker, wrote the foundational feminist text "The Second Sex."

In the visual arts, Paul Cézanne was a pivotal Post-Impressionist painter whose work influenced the development of Cubism, and Marc Chagall, though born in the Russian Empire, became a naturalized French citizen and is known for his dreamlike, colorful modernist paintings. In performance, Josephine Baker, an American-born entertainer who became a French citizen, was a celebrated singer and dancer as well as a member of the French Resistance during World War II and, much later, in 2021, became the first Black woman inducted into France's Panthéon in recognition of her wartime service and civil rights activism; Édith Piaf remains France's most iconic popular singer, famous worldwide for songs such as "La Vie en rose."

Chapter 5: Living in French Society

5.1 Emergency Services and Healthcare

France maintains a set of dedicated emergency telephone numbers, each connected to a specific service: 15 reaches the SAMU (Service d'Aide Médicale Urgente), the medical emergency service, for serious health emergencies; 17 reaches the police; and 18 reaches the fire department (sapeurs-pompiers), who in France also respond to many medical emergencies and accidents, not just fires. The single European emergency number, 112, can also be dialed from anywhere in France (or the EU) and will connect the caller to the appropriate service; this number is useful for visitors or residents unsure which specific number to use.

France's healthcare system is built around a universal public health insurance scheme, the Sécurité sociale, financed through payroll and other contributions, which reimburses residents for a substantial share of their medical costs. Every insured person carries a Carte Vitale, a green electronic card that identifies them within the health insurance system and allows pharmacies, doctors, and other providers to process reimbursement claims automatically rather than requiring paper forms. Reimbursement claims themselves are handled by the CPAM (Caisse Primaire d'Assurance Maladie), the local branch of the national health insurance fund; many residents also subscribe to a complementary private insurance plan (mutuelle) that covers costs the basic Sécurité sociale reimbursement does not.

For a non-urgent health issue, patients are expected to consult their médecin traitant, or attending/family physician, first. This doctor, chosen and declared by the patient to the health insurance system, coordinates the patient's overall care and issues referrals to specialists when needed; consulting a specialist without first going through the médecin traitant can result in a lower reimbursement rate. Hospital emergency rooms (urgences) are reserved for genuinely urgent or serious medical situations rather than routine care, precisely because the médecin traitant and local clinics are meant to handle non-urgent needs. Mandatory vaccinations, required for children in France, exist to protect both the vaccinated individual and the wider population by limiting the spread of dangerous infectious diseases (a principle known as collective or "herd" immunity).

5.2 Family and Civil Status

In France, only a civil marriage performed by a registrar (officier d'état civil) at the town hall (mairie) is legally recognized as a marriage; a religious ceremony alone, without this civil step, has no legal effect under French law, though many couples choose to hold a religious ceremony in addition to the mandatory civil one. When a child is born in France, the birth must be declared to the civil registry office at the town hall within five days of the birth, a legal obligation placed on the parents (or whoever attended the birth) that creates the child's official birth certificate and legal identity.

Parental authority (autorité parentale) in French law is not simply a set of privileges over a child but a bundle of duties: parents are legally obligated to protect their child's safety, health, morality, and education, and to ensure the child's development, taking the child's own views into account as appropriate to their age. This legal framework underlies France's approach to child welfare and explains why authorities can intervene if parents fail to meet these obligations.

5.3 Work and Employment

French labor law sets a statutory legal working week of 35 hours for full-time employees, a standard introduced in stages beginning in the late 1990s and early 2000s; hours worked beyond this threshold are generally paid at an increased overtime rate or compensated with additional time off, subject to sector-specific agreements. Every employer must pay at least the SMIC (salaire minimum interprofessionnel de croissance), France's legally guaranteed minimum wage, which is reviewed and typically increased each year; employers cannot legally set wages below this floor regardless of sector.

Undeclared work (travail non déclaré, or travail dissimulé) — employing someone, or working, without officially declaring the job and paying the associated social contributions — is illegal in France and can result in significant fines and other penalties for employers, precisely because it deprives workers of protections such as health coverage, unemployment insurance, and pension rights, and deprives the state of tax revenue used to fund public services. The first step recommended for anyone looking for a job in France is to register with France Travail (the public employment service, formerly known as Pôle emploi), which helps match jobseekers with openings, provides training programs, and administers unemployment benefits for those who qualify.

Both men and women have equal rights to start and run a business in France, and a foreign national may also create a business, provided their residence permit authorizes them to carry out a professional or commercial activity — some residence permits restrict holders to salaried employment only, while others (or specific business-creation visas) explicitly authorize self-employment or company formation. Minors may generally begin working from age 16, though French law permits limited, lighter forms of work from age 14 under strict conditions, typically during school holidays and with parental authorization.

5.4 Education

Schooling in France is compulsory (obligatoire) for every child, French or foreign, residing in the country, from age 3 through age 16 — a range extended downward from age 6 to age 3 by a 2019 reform, reflecting the importance French policy places on early education. This obligation applies regardless of the family's immigration status, and a child enrolled in school is expected to attend regularly (assiduité). Children who do not yet speak French upon arrival are not excluded from school; instead, they are enrolled in the ordinary school system and given additional, specialized language support — commonly through dedicated units called UPE2A (unités pédagogiques pour élèves allophones arrivants) — to help them learn French and integrate into mainstream classes as quickly as possible.

After the compulsory, free elementary school (école élémentaire) stage, French students move on to the collège (lower secondary / middle school, roughly ages 11-15), and afterward to the lycée (upper secondary / high school, roughly ages 15-18), which may be general, technological, or vocational in orientation. At the end of the lycée, students who succeed in the corresponding national exam are awarded the baccalauréat ("le bac"), the diploma that both certifies completion of secondary education and grants access to university-level study in France.
